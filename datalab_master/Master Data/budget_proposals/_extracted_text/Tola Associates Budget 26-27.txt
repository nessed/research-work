BUDGET 2026–27: PATHWAY TO ECONOMIC SOVEREIGNTY AND
IMF EXIT
Pakistan's fiscal outlook for Fiscal Year 2026-27 (“FY27”) faces various macroeconomic challenges,
including post-Gulf war issues, weaker tax collection, and ongoing talks with the International Monetary
Fund (“IMF”) under its stabilization program. While the Government is committed to keeping fiscal
discipline and aiming for a sizable primary surplus, ongoing structural issues in revenue collection- coupled
with ambitious IMF targets- continue to present risks to fiscal stability.
So far, under the $7 billion IMF program, Pakistan has received $3 billion, with the next $1 billion tranche
expected in early May. Additionally, in late March 2026, an agreement was reached with the IMF for an
extra $1.2 billion.
According to the article cited by the media1, this agreement involved adding 11 new conditions, bringing
the total to 75 over the past two years. These conditions cover a wide range of areas, including economic
decision-making, governance, and private sector growth.
The Government has also assured the IMF that Parliament will approve the FY27 budget in line with the
program's targets, just as it did with the previous budget. This is the second time under the current
programme that such a commitment has been made, as the previous budget was also approved in accordance
with IMF requirements.
The initial Federal Board of Revenue (“FBR”)’s tax target for FY26 was set at Rs. 14.13 trillion, later
revised downward to Rs.13.98 trillion during the second review, yet it remains largely unattainable. The
FBR is now seeking a further reduction to approximately Rs.13.5 trillion, reflecting persistent revenue
shortfalls. This underperformance is evident from the failure to meet two key indicative targets during July–
December FY26, including the Rs.6.5 trillion overall tax collection target and the Rs.366 billion retail sector
target.
Looking ahead, the IMF has set an ambitious tax goal of Rs. 15.6 trillion for FY27 and has suggested
measures like removing sales tax exemptions on fuel and new housing2. However, tax authorities believe
even reaching Rs.15 trillion will be challenging, primarily depending on this year's actual collections. The
IMF also recommends raising the tax-to-GDP ratio to 11.3%, an increase of about 0.3% (roughly Rs.400

In pursuing these loan tranches, Pakistan reached an agreement with the IMF on three policy actions:

Endorsing such policies would be disastrous for the economy. The estimated fair value of the Pakistani

over the past decade, remaining within a narrow range and failing to achieve sustained growth. While
exports peaked at $32.5 billion in FY22 (Rs. 177/USD), they stood at $32.4 billion in 2024 (Rs. 274/USD),
indicating that devaluation has not enhanced export performance.
The currency was devalued by Rs.25 as part of an agreement with the IMF, contributing approximately 5%
to inflation (based on the estimate that a Rs.10 devaluation increases inflation by 2%). This devaluation has
pushed the exchange rate well above its estimated fair value and is a significant factor behind the already
high inflation.
Devaluation also prompts an increase in interest rates. A 10% currency depreciation can lead to a 6% rise
in inflation, necessitating an equivalent 6% increase in interest rates to stabilize prices. This scenario would
entail roughly Rs.2 trillion in additional debt servicing costs (net of SBP profits), thereby slowing economic
growth and expanding the fiscal deficit.
Tax reform itself faces systemic resistance. Efforts to broaden the tax base, such as taxing sectors of the
undocumented economy like wholesalers and retailers, are frequently obstructed by political and elite
interests.

of the ongoing FY26, according to the Pakistan Bureau of Statistics. Compared with the previous quarter,
growth in the agriculture and industrial sectors has decelerated, while the services sector has increased.
Fundamentally, this underscores that although macroeconomic indicators, such as GDP growth, may appear

A. Reviving the Agriculture: Transforming the sector for a Sustainable Future
1. Crop Sector – From Stagnation to High-Value, Technology-Driven Agriculture
Pakistan’s agricultural sector is approaching a state of decline, as the primary crop industry has experienced
a double-digit contraction in recent years. Notably, the sector recorded a substantial decline of
approximately 13.5%, indicating a period of significant underperformance. According to the latest Labor
Force Survey (“LFS”), the employment rate within this sector also decreased from 37.1% in 2020-21 to
35.1% in 2024-25. Approximately 27.98 million individuals are employed in this sector.

Pakistan must fundamentally reconsider the utilization of the SIFC from an agricultural perspective. The
Land Information Management System (“LIMS”) should be effectively implemented, and the Real Estate
Investment Trust (“REIT”) concept should be promoted actively. Incentives should be provided to foreign

strengthen the Agro-based economy, and diminish dependence on the IMF.
The anticipated result would be an increased contribution of the agriculture sector to the GDP and a
corresponding rise in employment opportunities. Additionally, if an extra 3 million hectares of land are
cultivated this year expanding the current cultivated area of 22.27 million hectares as per the latest Pakistan
Bureau of Statistics data the sector’s contribution to GDP could increase to approximately 27%. It is
projected that such expansion would positively impact agricultural GDP growth by about 4-5%, alongside

Pakistan’s Agriculture sector must shift from fragmented, low-productivity farming to a more commercial,

to sustain and increase gains in agricultural productivity and rural livelihoods.
1.3. Eliminating Intermediaries (Arhtis) through Direct Bank Financing
All farms exceeding 100 acres should be restructured into shareholding companies through land conversion.
Financial institutions should provide direct credit to these entities, eliminating intermediary involvement.
Moreover, the SBP has previously introduced an initiative to facilitate direct credit transfer from banks to
farmers, thereby reducing reliance on intermediaries (arhti). This model should be expanded and
implemented on a larger scale to maximize its benefits.
Despite overcoming production challenges, farmers remain entangled with arhtis. Approximately 90% of
smallholders are affected, with 48% relying on intermediaries for inputs8. Farmers often take loans for seeds
and fertilizers at inflated prices and inferior quality and are compelled to sell their crops at low prices. This
perpetuates a cycle of debt in which middlemen profit twice—once from overpriced inputs and again from
underpriced outputs. Market reform requires dismantling the dominance of intermediaries through the

1.4. High-Value Crop Diversification Strategy
Pakistan must transition from low-productivity staples such as wheat and sugarcane toward high-value
crops, including oilseeds, pulses, horticulture, and nuts. Our neighboring country explicitly prioritizes crops
such as coconut, cocoa, cashew, and nuts to enhance farmer incomes and export potential.
Other crops include, jowar, barley, tobacco, oilseeds, pulses, vegetables, orchards, and miscellaneous crops.
Introduce minimum support prices (“MSP”) for oilseeds and pulses; establish export-oriented crop zones
(e.g., mango, rice, vegetables); and provide input subsidies linked to productivity rather than acreage. This
strategy will reduce the import bill particularly for edible oils such as palm oil and soya bean oil, which
account for 6% of total imports and increase exports and farmer incomes.
1.5 Climate-Resilient Agriculture
Concurrently, promote crop diversification and mixed cropping to diminish reliance on climate-sensitive
staples such as wheat. Introduce and subsidize climate-smart seed varieties that are heat-resistant, drought-
tolerant, and flood-resilient. Establish localized seed banks to ensure timely access to adaptive crops in
vulnerable regions, notably the Barani and Sailabi areas. Invest in climate-adaptive irrigation systems,
including small reservoirs and water-harvesting techniques, for flood- and drought-prone areas.

2.1 Livestock Commercialization and Export Strategy
Pakistan possesses one of the largest livestock populations globally; however, its export performance in
meat, dairy, and value-added products remains below potential. Over the past three years, Pakistan’s meat
exports have stagnated at approximately 100-123 thousand metric tons, hindered by sector fragmentation,
poor breed quality, inadequate cold chain infrastructure, and limited access to international markets. Last
year, beef exports contracted, with growth over the past five years totaling only 1.75%. According to the
official Indian Ministry of Finance, India exported meat worth approximately $4.15 billion in FY25,
whereas Pakistan’s exports were significantly lower at around $495 million.
2.1.1 Establish Corporate Dairy and Meat Farms
Initiate cluster-based livestock zones that integrate small farmers into value chains alongside larger farmers
who import superior-quality breed semen. Promote indigenous breeds and invest in their marketing.
2.1.2. Create Halal Meat Export Zones (CPEC-Linked)
Pakistan has a strong comparative advantage in Halal meat exports, particularly to the Middle East, Central
Asia, and China.
Develop designated Halal meat processing and export zones near ports such as Karachi and Gwadar, and
along CPEC routes; ensure compliance with international sanitary and phytosanitary (SPS) standards; and
establish modern slaughterhouses, meat processing facilities, and certification systems.

2.1.3. Develop Integrated Cold Chain Infrastructure
Currently, Pakistan loses 15–20% of its milk production due to the lack of cold chain systems.
Consequently, Pakistan produces only 58.2 million tons of milk, compared to India’s 248 million tons.
● Invest in cold storage, refrigerated transportation, and milk chilling centers.
● Introduce milk collection networks in rural areas.
● Encourage private sector investment through subsidies and concessional financing.
2.1.4. Credit-Linked Subsidy and Breeding Programs
A primary constraint in Pakistan’s livestock sector is limited access to affordable financing and poor genetic
quality of animals. India’s model of credit-linked subsidies and breeding programs offers a robust
framework that Pakistan can adapt to improve productivity and rural incomes. Implement low-interest loans
(3–5%) through commercial banks and microfinance institutions, and link subsidies to performance
indicators such as milk yield, herd size, and overall productivity.
3. Fisheries Sector – Blue Economy Transformation
Similar to the beef export scenario, fish meat exports have remained stagnant, with Pakistan exporting no
more than 200,000 metric tons of fish over the past five years. One of the primary issues is the loss of 15-
20% of fish stock due to inadequate cold chain infrastructure and inefficient handling processes9. Globally,
fish and fish products are among the most traded food commodities, reaching approximately 230 countries,
with a total value of US$195 billion in 2022. The average per capita consumption was 20.7 kilograms and
is expected to increase further10.
According to the ‘Blue Economy under URAAN Pakistan 2026’ initiative, the major challenges include
insufficient icing facilities, unhygienic conditions at landing sites, and the use of contaminated water for
ice production.
The National Aquaculture Mission (Pakistan) seeks to develop inland fisheries, reservoirs, and aquaculture
clusters, drawing inspiration from India’s plan to develop 500 reservoirs. Additionally, efforts to develop
the fisheries value chain focus on investments in cold chain infrastructure, processing capabilities, and

4. Forestry Sector – Climate and Revenue Dual Strategy
Pakistan is experiencing a severe rate of deforestation, with an estimated loss of 11,000 to 27,000 hectares
of forest each year. Currently, less than 5% of the country’s total land area is covered by forests, and around
1.5% of this already limited forest cover is being lost annually11. This ongoing deforestation poses serious
threats to biodiversity, environmental sustainability, and agricultural productivity.
The Commercial Forestry and Agroforestry Policy aim to incentivize private sector plantations, including
timber, bamboo, and sandalwood, inspired by India’s sandalwood promotion strategy. Additionally, the
development of a carbon credit market seeks to monetize forest resources through carbon trading
mechanisms. The agroforestry approach emphasizes integrating trees, crops, and livestock to promote
income diversification. Moreover, the strategy to substitute imports of timber aims to reduce reliance on
foreign supplies by expanding domestic forestry resources.

3. Mining and Minerals – Untapped Growth Engine
Currently, the industry contributes just 0.15% to global mineral output and 2–3% to the domestic GDP. Of
the 92 documented minerals in the country, nearly 90% remain unexplored12.
Pakistan is endowed with substantial mineral resources, with an estimated potential ranging between USD
6 trillion and USD 8 trillion13. A significant proportion of these reserves are located in Baluchistan, which
hosts some of the nation's most promising mining sites. Notably, substantial gold and copper deposits have
been identified in Dasht-e-Kuhn and Nokundi in the Chagai district, both of which are regarded as high-
potential regions for large-scale mineral extraction and downstream processing.
The Government ought to implement an Integrated Land Utilization and Investment Policy, whereby land
designated for mining, especially in Baluchistan, Thar, and Chagai regions, will be concurrently developed
for mineral extraction, corporate agriculture, and structured real estate development. Such a policy would
guarantee the continuity of economic activity beyond mining cycles, thereby preventing “ghost economies”
and fostering sustainable regional development.
Mining-Linked Special Economic and Agro Zones
Expedite high-impact projects such as the Reko Diq Project and Siah Diq deposits; expand Thar coal mining
operations for energy and industrial applications; and prioritize exploration in the Chagai region to unlock
future revenues.
Strategic Mega Projects Acceleration Policy
Fast-track high-impact projects such as the Reko Diq Project and Siah Diq deposits; Expand Thar coal
mining operations for energy and industrial use; Prioritize Chagai region exploration to unlock future
revenues.
Investment Promotion and Global Integration Policy

investment; build on the momentum generated by 5,000 delegates from over 50 countries and the signing

C. Service Sector
Considering the sector’s limited contribution to FBR tax revenues, targeted reforms are imperative to
improve compliance and documentation.
1. Wholesale and retailers
Rationalization of Sales Tax Rates: For enterprises integrated with Point of Sale (POS) systems, a 4%
reduction in sales tax rates may be implemented as an incentive for formalization. However, strict penalties
of up to 50% should be imposed for non-compliance or fraudulent activity to ensure enforcement and deter
tax evasion.
Market Efficiency and Transparency Policy: Establish a national supply chain data platform to monitor
prices, inventory levels, and demand patterns in real time. This initiative will enable policymakers to control
inflation and prevent hoarding. The mandatory adoption of digital invoicing and POS integration for large
retailers should be phased in for Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) to enhance economic
documentation. Enforcement against cartelization should be intensified through data analytics to detect
price manipulation within wholesale markets. Digitization of supply chains and inventory management
should be promoted by introducing ERP-lite systems and inventory-tracking tools for SMEs, alongside
fostering stronger integration between wholesalers and retailers to enhance efficiency and reduce
information asymmetries.

1.2 Amend Section 111 to allow taxation of undeclared benami (hidden) assets in the year they
are discovered, not in the year they were acquired. Further, the limitation period should
start from the year of discovery, not acquisition.
Example:
● Mr. A bought an undeclared benami property in 2012. This property was discovered by
tax authorities in 2025. Under current law, the tax officer may not be able to tax it, due
to the limitation period expiring. Under the proposed amendment, the assets would be
taxed in 2025 (year of discovery), and the limitation clock would start in 2025, not 2012.
1.3 Abolish Tajir Dost Scheme. Tax rate should be 1% across the board up to Rs.30 million.
Minimum tax should be applicable on the amount over and above to be charged on the
Reverse Charge Mechanism under Section 236G and 236H.
1.4 Rationalize minimum tax regime for commercial importers by increasing rates from 5.5%
to 7%, and from 3.5% to 5.0% for other importers. However, taxpayers should be allowed
to adjust any excess minimum tax paid under this section and the carry forward of excess
minimum tax shall be for the next five years.

the taxpayer. This will increase the reach to non-filers. If 7E is not withdrawn, then a tax
credit of 7E may be allowed against CGT arising from the disposal of the immovable
property.
1.6 Mandate advance tax payments under Sections 7E and CVT in two instalments based on
the prior year's tax liability, with an option to submit a justified estimate if the current
year’s liability is lower, ensuring at least 90% of the actual tax is paid in advance.
1.7 Increase the advance tax on prize bond winnings from 15% to 20%.
1.8 Introduce a limit for claiming interest expenses on foreign debt extended to other expenses
by amending Section 106A to prescribe a limit for claiming interest expenses on foreign
debt. It is recommended to include other expenses claimed by multinationals being remitted
to their affiliates, except for reimbursements of actual costs incurred by those affiliates to
third parties. This will discourage the remittance of exorbitant expenses by multinational
companies outside Pakistan.
1.9 In Pakistan, the definition of a "resident individual" for tax purposes is outlined under
Section 82 of the Income Tax Ordinance, 2001. An individual is considered a resident for
Page | 21


===== PAGE 25 =====
a particular tax year if they are present in Pakistan for 183 days or more during that year.
However, this definition is outdated and lacks alignment with more nuanced residency
rules in other jurisdictions.
Pakistan is recommended to modernize its residency criteria to reflect actual economic
presence and intent. A more appropriate definition will treat an individual as a Resident if
they stay in Pakistan for 182 days or more in a financial year. For those who have stayed
between 120 and 181 days, the status depends on their citizenship and income. For instance,
a Pakistani citizen as defined under the Pakistan Citizenship Act, 1951 or a person
possessing a Pakistan Origin Card (POC) with income exceeding a prescribed threshold
(excluding foreign-sourced income) and has no tax liability in any other country should be
treated as a Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident (RNOR). Those who do not meet these
conditions should be classified as non-residents. Individuals who spend less than 120 days
in Pakistan during the financial year should be treated as non-residents, regardless of
income or nationality.
1.10 It is proposed that tax reforms be introduced for salaried individuals such that, for those
earning up to Rs.2,400,000 annually (i.e., Rs.200,000 per month), a nominal tax of Rs.100
per month (Rs.1,200 per annum) be levied. For income above this threshold, the tax slab
should be capped at a maximum of 30%.
1.11 It is proposed that the threshold for employer contributions to a recognized provident fund,
as specified under Part I of the Sixth Schedule, be revised. Currently, employer
contributions exceeding one-tenth of the employee’s salary or Rs. 150,000, whichever is
lower. are treated as income received by the employee. It is recommended that this
threshold be enhanced from Rs. 150,000 to Rs. 1,500,000, while retaining the existing limit
of 10% salary. The last revision to this contribution limit was made through the Finance
Act, 2016, and it has remained unchanged since then. An upward revision is therefore
necessary to align with current economic conditions and to encourage long-term retirement
savings.

What India's Budget Tells Pakistan About Its Own Tax Reform Agenda
A comparative policy note drawing on the Finance Bill 2026-27 (India)
India's Union Budget 2026-27 is not just a statement of fiscal intent for a neighboring economy; it is a
mirror. Many of the reforms tabled in the Finance Bill are responses to problems that Pakistan's tax system
either shares or has left unaddressed for longer. Reading it carefully, two things become apparent: some of
India's new measures would transplant to Pakistan's legal framework with relatively modest drafting effort,
and others point toward structural reforms that Pakistan needs to originate on its own terms.

Act, 1990, or exist in a form too weak to be meaningful. In each case, what India has introduced offers a
